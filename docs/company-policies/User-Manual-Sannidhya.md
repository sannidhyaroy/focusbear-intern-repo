## How I Work Best

I do my best work when I'm genuinely curious about the problem or find it meaningful. Interest and curiosity are my strongest motivators, so when I care about something, I naturally go deep. I also work well under clear deadlines and high stakes, which tend to sharpen my focus significantly.

The flip side of curiosity is that it can pull me deeper into rabbit holes than the task strictly requires. I'm aware of this, and deadlines help me self-correct. When the stakes are clear and time is bounded, my focus and absorption tend to sharpen quite a bit. That deadline-driven productivity boost is real and effective, but it's also very draining. After an intense sprint, I tend to need some recovery time before I'm fully back to normal capacity.

Understanding the "why" behind a task genuinely helps me engage with it better, especially for things that feel disconnected from a larger purpose.

A clean and organized workspace helps me a lot. A cluttered environment puts a subtle but real weight on my mind, so I tend to clear everything I don't need before settling into work. Deep work sessions where focus can lock in are where I'm most productive. Structured to-do lists help, though if one doesn't exist my brain tends to create a mental map on its own anyway.

Working alongside others who are also focused can help me naturally put in my best effort without needing to consciously motivate myself. This only works when I'm genuinely comfortable and casual with the person though. With strangers or in formal settings, it has the opposite effect.

## Meetings and Live Communication

Live calls cost me noticeably more cognitive energy than text-based communication, and it's not disengagement. Before joining calls and during certain moments in calls, my nervous system tends to go into high-alert mode like heart rate spikes, and a significant portion of my mental bandwidth goes into just staying composed and managing how I'm coming across in real time. It's not a choice, it just happens, and whatever cognitive bandwidth is left after all of that is what actually goes into the conversation.

In high-pressure moments, my responses may feel rushed or compressed. My brain tends to fill silences immediately even if what comes out isn't my clearest thinking. The anxiety of a growing gap overrides the thinking itself, so I'll say something rather than nothing, just not always my best something.

I find it easier when the environment feels casual and low-pressure. Lower stakes help more of my actual thinking come through.

## Collaboration

I work best when expectations are communicated clearly. Ambiguity tends to make me anxious, not because I can't handle uncertainty, but because my mind subconsciously fills gaps with worst-case assumptions rather than best-case ones.

What helps most is direct communication, written instructions where possible, and minimal context-switching interruptions during focus hours.

I'm collaborative and willing to ask for help, though it sometimes takes me a moment to do so. I'd rather understand something properly than pretend I do.

---

## Feedback

I genuinely want honest feedback. I'd rather know clearly if something isn't right than be left guessing. Transparency is something I deeply value.

My mind automatically tends to replay interactions and think about them afterward, sometimes more than necessary. So while I'm composed externally, feedback lands more deeply internally than it might appear. I'm not fragile, but I do process things thoroughly. Written feedback gives me the space to do that without added pressure.

## Quirks and Things to Know

- In live situations, my brain responds immediately rather than pausing to think. Any thinking done is real-time with whatever cognitive bandwidth is available, not pre-processed. So what comes out might be compressed or imperfect, not because I don't know, but because the mental processing happens on the fly.
- My mind tends to think through edge cases and worst-case scenarios before they happen. This makes me thorough, though it occasionally tips into overthinking.
- I care a lot about getting things right, sometimes more than getting things done fast. I'm working on finding the balance.
- I process ideas slowly. I often brainstorm by speaking out loud to myself solo, because hearing my own reasoning helps my brain make sense of it. I'll brainstorm in text too, but call meetings are where this comes least naturally.
- I work better with written instructions than verbal ones. I can refer back to them and process at my own pace.
- I prefer understanding the full context of a task before starting rather than figuring it out as I go, because it gives me clarity and reduces the dread of the unknown.
