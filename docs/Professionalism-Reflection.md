# Professionalism Reflection

## Research & Learning

### Professional vs. Unprofessional Behaviour in a Casual Work Environment

Professional behaviour in a casual environment like Focus Bear isn't about
formality. It's about reliability, respect, and accountability.

Professional:
- Communicating clearly and proactively without waiting to be chased
- Coming to meetings prepared with notes, questions, and an understanding of
  current progress
- Following up on action items and not repeating the same questions
- Escalating blockers appropriately rather than going silent
- Taking initiative when unsure instead of waiting for instructions
- Giving regular updates even when there's nothing dramatic to report

Unprofessional:
- **Ghosting:** going silent without explanation
- Coming to meetings without having read relevant materials
- Asking questions that a quick search would answer
- Ignoring feedback or letting the same issues repeat without addressing them
- Shipping or submitting work you don't actually understand
- Using vague calendar invites or subject lines that give no context

The key distinction in a casual environment is that professionalism is measured
by how reliably and respectfully you show up, not by how formally you dress or
speak.

### Respectful Communication in Remote Teams

Remote communication loses a lot of the context that in person communication
provides: tone, body language, and facial expressions don't travel over text.
This means:

- Assume good intent by default. A short or blunt message is usually just someone being efficient, not being rude.
- Be clear and specific rather than vague. "Can you review this when you get a chance?" is better than "Hey, can you check something?"
- Match urgency to the right channel. Email for general things, Discord for medium priority, SMS for urgent, call for genuine emergencies.
- Follow up if you don't get a response. Nudges are welcome and expected, not annoying.
- Don't multi task during meetings. If you're there, be there.

### Giving and Receiving Feedback Professionally

Giving feedback:
- Focus on the action or output, not the person
- Be specific about what could be improved and why
- Assume the person had good intent and frame accordingly

Receiving feedback:
- Listen fully before responding
- Ask for clarification rather than reacting defensively
- Take notes so the same feedback doesn't have to be repeated
- Act on it, as the most professional response to feedback is visible change

## Reflection

### Great Teamwork and Professionalism

The most effective teamwork I've observed or experienced shares a common trait:
clarity without rigidity. People know what they're responsible for, communicate
when something changes, and don't leave others guessing. What makes it work
isn't necessarily people being formal or structured, it's people being reliable
and honest. You can be casual and professional at the same time as long as
people can count on you.

### Making Communication Clear, Respectful, and Inclusive

My natural default is written async communication, which already helps with
clarity. A few specific things I want to be mindful of:

- Being explicit about context rather than assuming shared understanding
- Not letting ambiguity sit, if something is unclear, asking early rather
  than guessing and going in the wrong direction
- Being patient with different communication styles, especially in a team
  where several members are neurodivergent and may communicate or process
  differently than I expect

### Creating a Positive and Professional Work Environment

Mostly by staying reliable and not adding to anyone's cognitive load
unnecessarily. Showing up to standups prepared, keeping updates regular,
and not making teammates chase me for information. Focus Bear's culture is
already casual and supportive, so matching that energy while staying accountable
is the balance I want to maintain.

### Preparing Effectively for Meetings

From the supervisor guide and my own onboarding experience:

- Read any relevant briefs or materials before the meeting, not during it.
- Know my current progress and any blockers going in
- Have questions ready and make sure they're questions that aren't basic enough that I could answer with a quick search
- Bring a loose agenda if I'm the one who called the meeting
- Take notes during so I'm not asking the same things twice

### Being Proactive During the Internship

Proactive means not waiting to be told what to do next. When I finish a task,
the next step is to look at the milestone, identify what makes sense to work
on, and either proceed with a note to the supervisor or check in with something
like "Based on what I've done, I think X makes sense next. Does that sound
right?" That way I'm moving forward without going rogue.

Regular end of day updates are something I want to build as a habit, even
when progress feels small. A quick "worked on X, planning Y tomorrow" is
enough to keep everyone in sync.

### Following Up and Escalating

From the supervisor guide, the escalation ladder is:
- Email for general things, expect a reply within 48 hours
- Discord for medium priority, expect a reply within 24 hours
- SMS for urgent things needing a response within 2 hours
- Call for genuine emergencies only

If I don't get a response, a nudge is always appropriate. Most things aren't
urgent, so starting with the lightest touch and escalating only if genuinely
needed is the right default. Escalating too quickly creates noise. Not
escalating when something is actually blocking creates delays.

### Things I Won't Do

Two things that are specific to my own values and work style:

- I won't ship or submit something I don't understand. If I can't explain what
a piece of code does or defend a decision I made, it doesn't go out. This
applies to AI generated code especially, as I review, and understand before anything gets committed.

- I won't throw basic questions at the team without doing my own research first.
I'll search, read documentation, try things, and only ask when I'm genuinely
stuck after giving it a real attempt. The team's time is better spent on
questions that actually need their input.
